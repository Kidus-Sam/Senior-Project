Project Summary
AudioTexture is a specialized desktop application designed to classify musical audio through the analysis of acoustic texture using deep learning. While traditional music categorization often suffers from the inconsistencies and subjectivity of manual tagging, this system leverages a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on the FMA small dataset—which consists of 8,000 balanced tracks across eight distinct genres—to identify musical styles through objective spectral analysis. By automating metadata generation, the application ensures that independent musicians can precisely tag their work, overcoming the categorization hurdles often encountered during digital distribution on major streaming platforms.

The application features a responsive graphical user interface (GUI) developed with Tkinter that supports seamless drag-and-drop functionality for raw audio files. Upon upload, the system processes the signals into high-resolution Mel-spectrograms, which act as visual "fingerprints" representing frequency intensity over time. These images are analyzed by the CNN to determine the most probable genre, such as Soul, Hip-Hop, or Electronic, and provide a corresponding confidence percentage to indicate prediction reliability. This content-based methodology allows for a purely objective classification based on auditory data, effectively eliminating the biases and errors associated with subjective human labeling.
Significance	
AudioTexture addresses the "Black Box" crisis in the global music industry, where approximately $15 billion in royalties go undistributed annually due to incorrect or missing metadata. Independent artists are often "erased" by streaming algorithms when their work is mislabeled. For instance, artists like Kaytranada are frequently tagged under "Electronic" instead of the R&B charts where they reside, while global artists like Tems are often generalized as "World Music," limiting their commercial reach.

From a Computer Science perspective, this project involves complex signal processing, high-dimensional data management, and the implementation of deep learning architectures. The complexity lies in converting non-visual audio data into a format suitable for computer vision and optimizing a CNN to distinguish between nuanced musical textures. This bridges the gap between digital signal processing (DSP) and Machine Learning, requiring a rigorous understanding of both feature extraction and neural network optimization.

Technical Details
AudioTexture implements a sophisticated deep learning pipeline to bridge raw audio signals and classification results. The implementation utilizes the FMA small dataset, which consists of 8,000 tracks organized in a hierarchical directory structure (folders 000 through 155). The system uses the tracks.csv metadata file to map these files to their respective labels using the Pandas library for efficient data manipulation.
1.	Preprocessing and Signal Processing
a.	The primary challenge of this project is converting time-series audio data into high-quality visual data suitable for computer vision. I will use the librosa.feature.melspectrogram function to achieve this.
b.	FFT Parameters: I have chosen a window size FFT of 2048 and a hop length of 512. These parameters provide an optimal balance between time and frequency resolution, allowing the system to capture both sharp rhythmic transients (essential for genres like Hip-Hop) and sustained harmonic textures (essential for Soul).
c.	Mel-Scale Conversion: The resulting power spectrogram is converted to the Mel-scale, which non-linearly maps frequencies to better mimic human auditory perception. Finally, the amplitude is converted to decibels (log-scale) to ensure the features are normalized for the neural network.
d.	File Structure: Preprocessed spectrograms will be saved as image files to reduce the overhead of re-calculating the FFT during the iterative model training process on Google Colab.
2.	Model Architecture
The core of AudioTexture is a Sequential Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The following architecture is currently proposed to balance depth with local inference speed; however, this design is subject to change as the model has not yet been empirically tested against the specific nuances and auditory features of the FMA small dataset.
a.	Convolutional Layers: The model will feature three distinct Conv2D layers with 32, 64, and 128 filters respectively, each using a 3 X 3 kernel. These layers act as automated feature extractors, identifying low-level edges and high-level patterns within the spectral imagery.
b.	Regularization: Each convolutional layer is followed by a Batch Normalization layer and a ReLU activation function. To mitigate the high risk of overfitting on the relatively small 8,000-sample dataset, I will implement Dropout layers (with a rate of 0.3) after the Max-Pooling stages and the final dense layers.
c.	Output Stage: The final architecture includes a Global Average Pooling layer or a Flatten layer followed by two Dense layers. The final output layer uses a Softmax activation function to generate a probability distribution across the eight target genres.


3.	Training and Optimization
a.	Training will be conducted in a GPU-accelerated Google Colab environment to significantly reduce the time required for backpropagation.
b.	Optimization: I will use the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.001. Adam is chosen for its adaptive learning rate capabilities, which typically leads to faster convergence in audio classification tasks compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Descent.
c.	Loss Function: The system uses Categorical Crossentropy as the loss function, which is the industry standard for multi-class classification problems.
d.	Evaluation Tradeoffs: I will evaluate the tradeoff between model complexity and responsiveness. A deeper model might provide a 2-3% increase in accuracy but could cause the local Tkinter GUI to lag during inference. My priority is maintaining a response time of under 3 seconds on a consumer-grade CPU, which may require pruning the network if initial benchmarks are too slow.

Future Enhancements
•	Multi-label Tagging for Hybrid Genres: Modern music increasingly blurs traditional boundaries, resulting in hybrid styles such as Jazz-Fusion, Trap-Soul, or Psychedelic Rock. The current architecture is proposed to employ a "winner-take-all" softmax activation, but a future iteration would utilize sigmoid activation functions on the output layer to allow for multi-label classification. This would enable the system to assign multiple genre tags to a single track with independent confidence scores, providing a more nuanced and accurate acoustic profile for complex compositions that do not fit into a single category.
•	Mood and BPM Detection: Beyond broad genre classification, independent musicians require granular metadata for professional synchronization and playlisting. This enhancement involves extending the CNN to detect emotional "valence" (e.g., happy, sad, aggressive) and rhythmic "energy" levels. Additionally, implementing an algorithmic Beats Per Minute (BPM) counter would significantly increase the utility of the application for DJs and music supervisors who rely on tempo matching for professional mixes and licensing opportunities.
•	Porting to a Mobile-Friendly Web API: While the current version is a localized desktop application, migrating the inference engine to a cloud-based web API would drastically increase accessibility. By utilizing a web framework such as FastAPI or Flask, the model could be hosted on a remote server, allowing users to upload audio via a mobile-friendly web interface. This architectural shift would move the heavy computational load of the CNN to the cloud, enabling musicians to tag their demos instantly from any device without requiring high-performance local hardware.

What do you need to think about ahead of time in order to mitigate ethical concerns? What do you need to acknowledge could be an ethical concern? 

Example:
In developing AudioTexture, it's essential to acknowledge the ethical implications, particularly concerning algorithmic bias and dataset representation to ensure fairness, transparency, and responsible use of the platform.
•	Transparency and Accountability: AudioTexture will prioritize transparency by providing clear explanations of its functioning to users. It will be made explicit that the platform serves as an advisory tool and reference, offering genre predictions and acoustic insights based on historical audio data and AI algorithms. The platform will empower users to use the information provided as a reference point and not as an absolute decision-making tool. Users will be informed that these predictions may not always reflect the artist's original intent or cultural nuances, especially given that training data is influenced by specific dataset curation and Western-centric musical labeling.
•	Risk Disclosure: AudioTexture will include a disclaimer stating that while the platform aims to provide valuable insights into musical texture, it does not guarantee industry success or royalty accuracy. Users will be reminded that automated tagging carries inherent risks of misrepresentation, particularly if the music being analyzed deviates significantly from the genres represented in the FMA small dataset. Decisions regarding official metadata and distribution should not solely rely on AI-generated predictions.
•	AI Algorithm Transparency: AudioTexture will explain how the AI algorithms work in non-technical terms to enhance transparency. Specifically, it will explain how audio is converted into a visual "spectrogram" and how a neural network identifies visual patterns to estimate a track's genre.
